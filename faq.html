Encryption is a security measure that protects data by converting it into an unreadable format that only authorized users can decrypt. It ensures confidentiality and prevents unauthorized access.
Hacking, on the other hand, is the act of exploiting vulnerabilities in systems to gain unauthorized access, steal data, or disrupt operations.
While encryption strengthens security, hacking seeks to break or bypass it.

Use firewalls and intrusion detection systems to monitor and block threats.
Implement strong authentication and access controls (e.g., multi-factor authentication).
Encrypt sensitive data to protect it from unauthorized access.
Perform regular security audits and penetration testing to identify weaknesses.
Backup critical data to ensure recovery in case of an attack.

Phishing is a cyber attack where hackers send fraudulent emails or messages pretending to be from legitimate sources to trick users into revealing personal information like passwords, credit card details, or login credentials.

How to Protect Yourself:
Do not click on suspicious links or attachments.
Verify the sender's email address before responding.
Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) for extra security.
Use anti-phishing software and browser security features.
Educate yourself and employees on recognizing phishing attempts.

Phishing attacks: Hackers use fake messages to steal login credentials.
Malware infections: Downloading untrusted apps can introduce malicious software.
Public Wi-Fi threats: Unsecured networks can allow attackers to intercept data.
Outdated software: Lack of updates makes devices vulnerable to cyber threats. To mitigate these risks, users should install security updates, use strong passwords, enable encryption, and avoid untrusted networks and applications.

Ransomware is a type of malware that locks or encrypts your files, demanding payment (ransom) to restore access.

How to Prevent Ransomware Attacks:
Regularly backup your data to an external or cloud-based system.
Avoid downloading attachments or clicking on links from unknown sources.
Keep your software and security patches up to date.
Use strong, unique passwords and enable multi-factor authentication.
Deploy anti-malware and endpoint protection solutions.

Some of the most common cyber threats include:

Phishing Attacks - Fraudulent messages tricking users into giving up personal information.
Ransomware - Malware that locks files and demands a ransom for recovery.
Data Breaches - Unauthorized access to sensitive data, often leading to leaks.
DDoS Attacks - Hackers overwhelm a website/server with traffic to disrupt services.
Social Engineering - Manipulating people into revealing confidential information.
Insider Threats - Employees or contractors misusing access to steal or harm data.

Signs that your device may have been hacked include:

Unusual system behavior (e.g., slow performance, frequent crashes).
Pop-ups and unauthorized changes in settings.
Strange emails or messages sent from your account.
New or unknown software installed without your knowledge.
Unusual login attempts or security alerts from your accounts
